Are we on the brink of another global recession?

I've got a question for you: have you been feeling a little uneasy about the state of the world economy lately? If so, you're not alone. In fact, many experts believe that we're teetering on the edge of a global recession. Changes in leadership, Covid and Brexit are just a few of the challenges we have had to face over the past few years. It feels like one thing has led to another, but when do we have to sit down and worry about the state of the world economy?

The G7 countries (United States, Canada, Germany, France, Italy, Japan, and the United Kingdom) are among the largest and most influential economies in the world. When the economies of these countries are doing well, it is generally a positive sign for the global economy. On the other hand, if the economies of the G7 countries are experiencing a downturn, it can be a sign that the global economy is heading into a recession. Here, it is clear to see that the USA has had a constant trend on increasing consumption. However, this is not as much of a case for the other G7 countries. Consumption has been relatively stagnant for the past few years, which is a possible factor as to why a global recession could be on its way.

Happiness and economic growth are often thought to be correlated, but the relationship between the two is complex and can vary depending on a number of factors. Some research suggests that as countries become wealthier, their citizens tend to become happier, at least up to a certain point. This relationship is often referred to as the Easterlin Paradox , named after economist Richard Easterlin, who first identified it.

The graph I have created shows the relationship between the GDP per capita and the Happiness Index Score of the 50 "happiest" countries in the world. The happiness data was retrieved by data scraping a PDF from the World Happiness Report using python. The GDP per capita data was taken from the World Bank. I have also included population data which is shown by the size of the individual bubbles. It clear to see that, based on this data, the "happier" the country, the higher GDP per capita. This raises a possible argument that happiness correlates with GDP, meaning that, on a grander scale, should focus on health and wellbeing be of more importance, then countries will prosper hence reducing the risk of a global recession.
